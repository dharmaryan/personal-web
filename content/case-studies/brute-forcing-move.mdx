## June: deciding to YOLO my way into the US

I had wanted to move to the US for almost a decade. At this point, I had been screened out of several PE processes and had been increasingly interested in an operating role in tech, so I took control of the situation myself. 

At the end of June, I put in my 3 months' notice and left. 

### Designing my outbounding system

What began as manually crafting cold emails quickly became a system when I realised trying to send out 50 emails a day was not scalable. 

I procured Apollo for volume and deliverability, ChatGPT for templates and cross-referencing and five newly warmed emails forming the backbone for my outbounding. 

![Cold email template screenshot](/figure1-email-template.png "Figure 1: A template I used – on placeholder names, of course.")

### Ideal company profile

- Seed to Series C startups
- Founded post-2020 / 2021 (hypergrowth signals)
- Backed by credible investors (Sequoia, a16z, ICONIQ, big tech venture arms, YC / top accelerators for seed-stage)
- Contacts who were decision-makers, not recruiters
- Role focus on strategy, operations, finance, and GTM / sales

### Operating cadence

- Peaked at ~250 emails/day
- 10–12 intro calls/week
- 20 minutes prep per intro call
- Iterated sequences whenever reply rate <10%
- Weekends reserved for recovery
- Gmail used to maintain organic deliverability

### Conversion rules

- If role open, ask immediately for a referral
- If no role open, build relationship and stay in touch
- Many “no role” conversations converted later

## July: gaining traction

It started slow but after a couple days, three calls came through and I accumulated a number of early interviews taken between 6-8am. At the same time, my previous employer had initiated the process of moving me to the US, but this process stalled by a full month in between interview rounds, so I dropped out and doubled down on outbound.

By the end of the month, I generated five live interview processes.

## August – September: securing my first offer and restarting outbound

While on holiday in Japan, a high growth AI hardware company asked me to fly to California on 24 hours notice for final round interviews. I completed a superday, received an offer and flew home with my job locked down. Felt great, right?

Well, a couple weeks later, the company retracted for company specific reasons so I had to rebuild the funnel on short notice. This time, I launched a second, much larger wave of outreach, generating 12 new active processes by the end of September when my notice period was done. 

## October: bouncing back and the matured funnel

With my last position wrapped up, I immediately booked a ticket to the US and left. I had also now progressed a number of these interviews to late stages and needed to close these out. I spent the next month flying between SF and NY and secured 2 offers before accepting my current role and dropping out of all other live processes.

The companies spanned developer infrastructure, AI workflow automation, AI search, fintech, GTM tooling, and analytics. I ultimately ended up in RevOps in my current position, because:

- The interview process completed in three days (positive signal for execution rigor)
- Founders had a very strong track record in scaling and exiting businesses
- Strong role fit – I was deep into learning about GTM and was looking for a high leverage operating role

## Key learnings

While these results were fairly strong for a first time outbound funnel, my TOFU volume definitely compensated for mistakes along the way. Some example learnings to keep in mind for conversion rates and broader context down the line:

- Don't accidentally auto-email multiple people at the same company when you're in a live interview process with them
- Simple things: clean company names before you load them into your outbound lists (no suffixes like "Inc", "LLC", "Acquired by") 
- Sometimes you'll get no responses and sometimes you'll get 3 interviews in an hour
- Each introductory call is two-way – it's better to burn an early lead rather than get dragged into a late stage process where there isn't a fit
- Listen to pain points and figure out how your experience can help solve these – strong alignment equals strong fit

## Ending remarks

The fact that this succeeded remains, to date, one of the achievements in my life that I’m most proud of. YOLO'ing my way into the US felt a bit arcane, but when you treat it like a sales motion, it's really just a volume game.

For the people that took the time out of their day to call me, thank you. For the people I unknowingly spammed, I apologise! 

And for people in my personal life trying to replicate this move, good luck!
