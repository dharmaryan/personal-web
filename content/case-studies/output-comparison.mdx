# An ex-financier’s view on why tech ships faster than finance (even at half the hours)

This is something I’ve always wanted to tease out. Since moving into an operations role, the concept that has come up most consistently is the idea of a system – not as this vague concept, but as something that fundamentally shapes output.

Well set-up systems are the reason small teams can outship entire organizations with a fraction of the headcount – and often a fraction of the hours.

## Definitions

- System: a collection of processes that automates a sequence of steps to reliably achieve a specific outcome, repeatedly and at scale.
- Finance: front-office investment banking, investing and deal-related roles 
- Tech: early, mid-stage and pre-IPO stage software (not legacy technology enterprises)

## The Unit Economics of Work

### Finance

In finance, output (measured by number of deals successfully completed) works something like this:

Output = [Hours x Skill x (1 – Fatigue) / Review] x (1 – Probability of Deal Dying)

Where:
-	Hours: total time spent constructing models, presentations and diligence deliverables
-	Skill: baseline workforce quality – this is usually very high in finance and not the binding constraint
-	Fatigue: cognitive load accumulation – increases superlinearly after ~8 hours
-	Review: the average # of review cycles before a deliverable is approved
-	Probability of Deal Dying: the likelihood that a deal dies for external reasons, even after substantial work has been completed (anyone who’s worked on live deals knows this)

Most of these factors are fixed in the short-run. Skill is fixed, review is structurally fixed due to the high opportunity cost of errors, and deal survival probability is exogenous. 

In practice, this means that finance scales output primarily by increasing hours, even when the marginal return on those hours decreases significantly with fatigue. Other components of the equation are either already optimised, slow to change or out of the team’s control.

This is where you get the stereotype of the analyst who worked an 18 hour day on 3 hours’ sleep!

### Tech

In tech, output works very differently because systems materially change the unit economics of work.

Once a system is designed and implemented, output is no longer tightly coupled to hours worked in a given week. Instead, effort is front-loaded into design, after which systems persist, compound and absorb scale.

A simplified representation looks something like this:

Output = [Hours x Skill x (1 – Fatigue) x System Leverage] / Review * (1 – Probability of Breakage)

Where:
-	Hours: now primarily spent on system design and iteration instead of repeated execution
-	Skill: baseline workforce quality, which is typically high but can be more variable across teams and stages
-	Fatigue: as defined above
-	System Leverage: the degree to which a system automates or eliminates manual steps
-	Review: as defined above
-	Probability of Breakage: the probability of a system malfunctioning due to bugs, edge cases or unintended changes in logic

Under this construct, skill and hours are reinvested into systems leverage, reducing the hours required to produce future output. The primary failure mode is breakage, which is endogenous. It arises from design choices, complexity and change velocity, and can be permanently reduced by engineering, testing and operational discipline. 

As a result, you can simultaneously have extremely hard-working start-up engineers building generational systems… and a Google SWE playing pickle ball who shipped his week’s deliverables in 20 hours. They can both be extremely productive.

Let’s walk through a worked example.

## Worked Example: Systematised GTM vs. Manual Deal Origination

### Systematised GTM

In a well-designed GTM system, outbound execution is driven by a data activation pipeline rather than individual judgement calls, which follow the below steps:

Process Flow:
1.	Accounts and leads are sourced programmatically based on defined ICP / criteria
2.	Records are enriched, deduped and validated automatically
3.	RevOps teams will enforce routing and rules of engagement across the system
4.	Messaging and sequencing logic is triggered based on data state
5.	Engagement and outcomes are captured automatically and fed back into the warehouse
6.	Reporting reflects reality without manual reconciliation
7.	The prospect receives a tailored email or message addressed to them from the seller

The important point is not any single tool or step, but the hand-offs between them. Entire categories of software exist to reduce friction at each transition, from systems of record to data warehouses to analytics and activation layers. 

There are even systems that allow cold outbound to be executed at scale. I’ve written about running one of these in practice [here].

Once all of this infrastructure exists, adding 10,000 additional prospects does not require 10x the effort. The system absorbs the scale.

### Manual Deal Origination

The closest parallel in finance to a systematised GTM pipeline is deal origination.

In practice, this process is far more human mediated and follows roughly the below steps:

Process Flow:
1.	A senior banker or investor defines a loose mandate (sector, revenue, EBITDA, geography)
2.	Analysts systematically screen large datasets using tools like S&P CapIQ and FactSet
3.	Screens output a long list of companies, usually hardcoded into Excel
4.	The list is manually filtered down through successive passes
5.	Senior team members source warm introductions through their networks
6.	Where warm intros don’t exist, outreach may fall back into cold emails. These are often copied and pasted into Microsoft Outlook and sent in batches
7.	Responses, follow-ups and outcomes are tracked manually in Excel or reconstructed after the fact

There is structure here, but it’s cognitive structure, not automation. Each step still requires manual human intervention, judgement and coordination.

As a result, output scales linearly with attention. Adding 10,000 additional companies requires close to 10x the analyst effort.

To be fair, this isn’t accidental either. The stakes are high, costs are asymmetric, and the companies involved are often orders of magnitude larger and more complex. Precision and discretion matter much more than throughput.

But – the economic consequence is clear. Unlike a systematised GTM pipeline, deal origination does not convert effort into durable infrastructure. Each cycle largely resets and has to be activated manually.

Having worked in both environments, this difference was really hard to articulate and really easy to feel. Finance is exceptional at teaching execution under pressure, learning how to push work through ambiguity and deliver when making mistakes is not an option. Tech, on the other hand, is exceptional at teaching automation – how to keep things working after you step away.

They’re both valuable skillsets but just optimised for different outcomes. Personally, if I can get the same amount done while working fewer hours, I’ll take that trade every time.
